Introduction/Motivation

Complex tensor operations have frequently appeared throughout various undergraduate classes (Math 321: Applied Mathematical Analysis 1, Physics 311: Classical Mechanics, Physics 322: Electromagnetic Fields, ME 363/573: Fluid and Computational Fluid Dynamics, EMA 521: Aerodynamics). Index notation (Einstein notation, Cartesian notation, etc.) is a tool for manipulating these equations into general arrays (tensors) via subscripts and a few unique symbols to simplify these operations and greatly reduce the complexity.

  • Scalar: A magnitude that does not change with a rotation of axes - A special tensor of order zero
  • Vector: Associates a scalar with a direciton - A special tensor of order one
  • Tensor: Associates a vector (or tensor) with a direction - A matrix is a special tensor of order two

    • Kronecker Delta Symbol: \(\delta_{ij} \)

    The Kronecker delta is a rank-2 symmetric tensor representing the dot product and is defined as follows:

    $$\delta_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } i = j \\ 0 & \text{if } i \neq j \end{cases}$$

    Alternatively, in matrix form:

    $$\delta_{ij} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

    Levi-Civita Symbol: \(\epsilon_{ijk} \)

    The alternating (or permutation) tensor is a rank-3 antisymmetric tensor representing the cross cross-product and is defined as follows:

    $$\epsilon_{ijk} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if (ijk) is an even (cyclic) permutation of (1,2,3)},\\ -1 & \text{if (ijk) is an odd (acyclic) permutation of (1,2,3)},\\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$ We can say that an even permutation is the same as a cyclic permutation. Likewise, we can say that an odd permutation is the same as an acyclic permuation. Symbolic representations of cylic and acyclic rotations are as follows respectively: $$ \epsilon_{123} = \epsilon_{231} = \epsilon_{312} = 1 $$ $$ \epsilon_{213} = \epsilon_{132} = \epsilon_{321} = -1 $$ The \( \epsilon_{ijk} \) symbol provides us with a compact expression for the components of the cross-product of the right-handed Cartesian basis: $$ \boxed{(\mathbf{e}_i \times \mathbf{e}_j) \cdot \mathbf{e}_k = \epsilon_{ijk}} $$ Similarly, we can also write the following since this is the k-component of \( (\mathbf{e}_i \times \mathbf{e}_j) \) $$ \boxed{(\mathbf{e}_i \times \mathbf{e}_j) = \sum_{k=1}^3 \epsilon_{ijk} \mathbf{e}_k} $$

    Sigma Notation, Free, and Dummy Indicies

    The expansion of vectors \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) in terms of the basis vectors \(\mathbf{e_1}\), \(\mathbf{e_2}\), \(\mathbf{e_3}\) is typically written as: \( \mathbf{a} = a_1\mathbf{e_1} + a_2\mathbf{e_2} + a_3\mathbf{e_3} \) and \(\mathbf{b} = b_1\mathbf{e_1} + b_2\mathbf{e_2} + b_3\mathbf{e_3} \). This can be compactly written using sigma notation. For example: $$ \mathbf{a} = \sum_{i=1}^3a_i\mathbf{e}_i, \hspace*{20pt} \mathbf{b} = \sum_{i=1}^3b_i\mathbf{e}_i $$ To perform basic vector operations, the Kronecker delta and Levi-Civita symbols will be employed for compactness. Note that these are only valid when the basis is orthonormal and right-handed. For example: $$ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \sum_{i=1}^3\sum_{j=1}^3a_ib_j\mathbf{e}_i\cdot\mathbf{e}_j = \sum_{i=1}^3\sum_{j=1}^3a_ib_j\delta_{ij} = \sum_{i=1}^3a_ib_i $$ $$ \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \sum_{i=1}^3\sum_{j=1}^3a_ib_j\mathbf{e}_i\times\mathbf{e}_j = \sum_{i=1}^3\sum_{j=1}^3\sum_{k=1}^3a_ib_j\epsilon_{ijk}\mathbf{e}_k $$ In the sums above, i and j are dummy/summation indicies which means they do not have a designated value; in fact, they have all values in their range (typically 1,2,3). We call them dummy indicies because it is their place and range that matter in the expression, NOT their assigned name. $$ \mathbf{a} = \sum_{i=1}^3a_i\mathbf{e}_i = \sum_{j=1}^3a_j\mathbf{e}_j = \sum_{k=1}^3a_k\mathbf{e}_k \neq \sum_{k=1}^3a_k\mathbf{e}_i $$ Here is an example of how indicies come in two kinds, dummies and free. $$ \mathbf{e}_i\cdot(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})\mathbf{c} = \Bigg(\sum_{j=1}^3a_jb_j\Bigg)c_i $$ In the above expression, the index i is free which means we can pick it for any value 1,2,3. Note that since we chose i to be the free index on the left-hand side of the equaiton, we must continue to use index i for \( c_i\) on the right-hand side. That leaves j, our dummy index, which is the index we are summing over.

    Einstein Summation Convention

    To reduce the amount of times we are writing out large sums, we will drop the \(\sum \) signs and adopt Einstein's summation convention which means that a repeated index implicitly denotes a sum over all values of that index. $$ \mathbf{a} = a_1\mathbf{e}_1 + a_2\mathbf{e}_2 + a_3\mathbf{e}_3 \equiv a_i\mathbf{e}_i $$ $$ \mathbf{a\cdot b} = a_ib_i $$ $$ \mathbf{a\times b} = \epsilon_{ijk}a_ib_j\mathbf{e}_k $$

    Dummy Repitition Rule: Indicies can never appear more than twice in the same term, and if they are, it is probably by mistake. Also, note that free indicies in different terms must match. Looking at the dot product again in index notation, the dummies must match as we can see. $$ \mathbf{a\cdot b} = (a_i\mathbf{e}_i) \cdot (b_j\mathbf{e}_j) = a_ib_j\mathbf{e}_i\cdot\mathbf{e}_j = a_ib_j\delta_{ij} = a_ib_i = a_jb_j $$

    Substitution Rule: If one of the indicies of \( \delta_{ij}\) is involved in a sum, we Substitute the summation index for the other index in \( \delta_{ij}\) and drop \( \delta_{ij}\), for example: $$ a_i\delta_{ij} \equiv a_1\delta_{1j} + a_2\delta_{2j} + a_3\delta_{3j} = a_j $$ If both indicies of \( \delta_{ij} \) are summed over as in the double sum \( a_ib_j\delta_{ij} \), it does not matter which index is being substituted, thus $$ a_ib_j\delta_{ij} = a_ib_i = a_jb_j $$ and likewise $$ \delta_{kl}\delta_{kl} = \delta_{kk} = \delta_{ll} = 3, \hspace*{20pt} \delta_{ij}\epsilon_{ijk} = \epsilon_{iik} = \epsilon_{jjk} = 0 $$ The results above are clearly true, but here is the noncompact form for validation: \( \delta_{kk} \equiv \delta_{11} + \delta_{22} + \delta_{33} = 1+1+1=3 \). As for the result of \( \epsilon_{ijk} \), the expression vanishes because two indicies have the same value. Remember that the cross-product measures how normal two vectors are. Since the resultant vector's magnitude corresponds to the area of the parallelogram formed by the two original vectors, it should make sense that two parallel vectors forms zero area. A useful identity that appears frequently in tensor calculus is \(\epsilon_{ijk}\epsilon_{klm} \). An excellent result (worth remembering) can be deduced as follows: $$ \begin{aligned} \epsilon_{ijk}\epsilon_{klm} &= \epsilon_{kij}\epsilon_{klm} = \begin{vmatrix} \delta_{kk} & \delta_{kl} & \delta_{km} \\ \delta_{ik} & \delta_{il} & \delta_{im} \\ \delta_{jk} & \delta_{jl} & \delta_{jm} \\ \end{vmatrix} \\ &= \delta_{kk}(\delta_{il}\delta_{jm} - \delta_{im}\delta_{jl}) - \delta_{kl}(\delta_{ik}\delta_{jm} - \delta_{im}\delta_{jk}) + \delta_{km}(\delta_{ik}\delta_{jl} - \delta_{il}\delta_{jk}) \\ &= \delta_{kk}(\delta_{il}\delta_{jm} - \delta_{im}\delta_{jl}) = \delta_{il}\delta_{jm} - \delta_{im}\delta_{jl} \end{aligned} $$ $$ \boxed{\epsilon_{ijk}\epsilon_{klm} = \epsilon_{k\textcolor{blue}i\textcolor{red}j}\epsilon_{k\textcolor{green}l\textcolor{purple}m} = \delta_{\textcolor{blue}i\textcolor{green}l}\delta_{\textcolor{red}j\textcolor{purple}m} - \delta_{\textcolor{blue}i\textcolor{purple}m}\delta_{\textcolor{red}j\textcolor{green}l}} $$ Here are some examples of what has been covered thus far: $$ \begin{aligned} \textcolor{blue}{\mathbf{e}_l\cdot(\mathbf{a\times b})} &= \epsilon_{ijk}a_ib_j\mathbf{e}_k\cdot\mathbf{e}_l = \epsilon_{ijk}a_ib_j\delta_{kl} \\ &= \epsilon_{ijl}a_ib_j = \textcolor{blue}{\boxed{\epsilon_{lmn}a_mb_n}} \\ \end{aligned} $$ $$ \begin{aligned} \textcolor{blue}{(\mathbf{a\times b})\times\mathbf{c}} &= \epsilon_{ijk}\epsilon_{klm}a_ib_jc_l \\ &= \epsilon_{kij}\epsilon_{klm}a_ib_jc_l = (\delta_{il}\delta_{jm} - \delta_{im}\delta_{jl})a_ib_jc_l \\ &= \delta_{il}\delta_{jm}a_ib_jc_l - \delta_{im}\delta_{jl}a_ib_jc_l = \textcolor{blue}{\boxed{a_ic_ib_m - a_mb_ic_i}} \end{aligned} $$
